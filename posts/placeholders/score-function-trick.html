The Score Function Trick and Why It Shows Up Everywhere

March 4, 2026

Here's a small identity that comes up constantly in variational inference, policy gradients, and diffusion models. It's worth knowing by heart.

The setup

Suppose you want to compute the gradient of an expectation with respect to the parameters of a distribution:

∇θ Ex ~ pθ[f(x)]

The trouble is that the distribution you're averaging over itself depends on θ. You can't just move the gradient inside the expectation.

The trick

Write the expectation as an integral and use the identity ∇θ pθ(x) = pθ(x) ∇θ log pθ(x), which follows from the chain rule. Then:

∇θ E[f(x)] = Ex ~ pθ[f(x) ∇θ log pθ(x)]

The gradient is now inside the expectation, and you can estimate it with Monte Carlo samples. The quantity ∇θ log pθ(x) is the score function (with respect to parameters), hence the name.

Where it shows up

  • REINFORCE / policy gradients: the policy gradient theorem is exactly this identity applied to the reward objective.
  • Variational inference: gradients of the ELBO with respect to variational parameters use the same trick (before reparameterization took over).
  • Score matching / diffusion models: the score ∇x log p(x) (now with respect to data, not parameters) is the central object. Different identity, same name, deeply related intuition.

A variance note

The score function estimator is unbiased but high variance. That's why control variates (baselines in RL) matter so much, and why the reparameterization trick — when applicable — is usually preferred. But when your distribution is discrete or the reparameterization isn't available, this is what you've got.
